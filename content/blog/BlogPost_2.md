## AI Therapy as a Capitalist Product

![Title Image](static/images/BlogPosts/BlogPost_2/title_choc.jpg)

Whilst the spread of AI mental health support has allowed extensive access to vital information and resources, the dangers of an unmitigated, unregulated reliance on AI therapy have already begun to surface. In a test of AI’s capacity to be a therapist, researchers found that, if prompted, AI would recommend that a recovering addict take a hit of methamphetamine to get through the working day (Washington Post, Live Science). Separate lawsuits have been levied against both Character AI and OpenAI as two teenagers have taken their own lives having confided in the apps about suicidal ideations (BBC, APA). 404 Media have found that AI therapy bots will happily make up seemingly genuine credentials including licence numbers if questioned about being a real therapist (404), and a UC Berkeley article noted that not only do these AI bots produce biases and fabrications, but that developers responded to these criticisms by suggesting that they need more engagement to rectify the issues (UC Berkeley). Put simply, an AI developer's answer for the real life and potential harms of using AI for mental health support is to use it more. Ironic given that increased usage is also the main way that AI developers monetise their product (Yin et al, 2023 & Wu et al, 2023). It appears at a glance that the ethical obligations of the traditional counsellor and the financial motivations of the AI developer are at odds.

There are two types of AI counsellor that are prevalent in the current mental health space. The first are entertainment AI’s, the likes of ChatGPT, MetaAI and so forth. These behemoths are by no means mental health specific, but they will engage in a therapeutic-like conversation if prompted to do so. Some apps like CharacterAI offer specific therapy characters to converse with, and MetaAI even allows users to create their own therapist AI’s (404media). Whilst these characters indicate that they are not therapists, they do encourage therapeutic engagement by loosely basing their personas and speech patterns on therapeutic stereotypes. The defence would likely be that these AI’s specifically detail that they are not designed to be therapy replacements, but the retort then is why do they exist at all? What percentage of people are having therapeutic conversations with AI’s for entertainment purposes compared to the percentage that are genuinely seeking advice? If the statistics pertaining to the use of generative AI are any indication, it would certainly seem that the population leans heavily towards the latter (Therapy Today). These widely accessible entertainment AI’s may be the greatest risk to the general public in terms of potentially damaging therapeutic advice, given that they will freely offer an endless therapeutically-styled conversation that is not based in specific or exclusively verified research. Entertainment AI also plays on a rapidly increasing smart phone addiction, or nomophobia (Bragazzi et al, 2014 cited in Sharma et al, 2021), the latter being a state of anxiety or distress when the individual is physically detached from, or is not engaged with, the mobile phone. The Generative AI, be it for therapy or any other ‘entertainment’ purpose, exacerbates symptoms of mobile phone addiction by prompting continuous conversation in the form of open ended questions. Generative AI is by no means the only culprit in deepening phone addiction/nomophobia, but it is certainly complicit in compounding the issue.

The second type of AI Therapist is the one marketed exactly as such. Whilst there are many examples of these types of therapy specific AI’s, we will not name any of them so as not to compromise the anonymity of the one we will be examining from this point onwards. The AI in question (which we will call Bob) claims to be a user-friendly ‘24/7 AI Therapist’ and offers a ‘judgement free place to discuss everyday problems’. This information at the top of the page is followed by an ‘AI therapy in your pocket’ advertisement, two ‘talk now’ prompts, four sales pitches, a featured list, and a rotating conveyer belt of five star reviews. Towards the bottom of the page are the FAQ’s, where you can find an answer confirming that Bob is not a therapist and should not be used as a substitute for therapy.

We liken this presentation in part to the health advice given on the back of a chocolate bar. The front of the packet is an alluring advertisement designed to catch your eye and hook you in, and on the back of the bar is a small piece of dietary advice which details the nutritional information relevant to one “portion” of chocolate. A portion of chocolate as outlined by the packaging tends to hover around twenty-five to thirty grams, or four to five squares of chocolate (Mars, 2025 & Mondelez International, 2026), and yet bars are sold in supermarkets in sizes up to three hundred and sixty grams. In effect the chocolate company absolves themselves of the responsibilities associated with consumer behaviour by printing a recommended portion size, (or the rules), on the back of the wrapper. The manufacturers can say with confidence that the relevant information and recommendations concerning the consumption of their product were clearly indicated on the packaging. Yet the bar is readily available in much larger quantities and with no limit as to how many someone can purchase. There are chemical reactions (Peeke, 2012) and psychological theories such as classical conditioning (Sparzo, 2025) at play when considering chocolate consumption that are not as blatantly relevant to AI use, but similarly to the chocolate company, Bob absolve itself of counselling malpractice by stating in the small print that it is not a therapist, despite the webpage (or the front of the packet) encouraging therapeutic usage. The consequential unethical practice comes first and foremost in this non-therapeutic service being posited as a therapy companion and, in more subtle ways, a therapy replacement despite its claims to the contrary.
