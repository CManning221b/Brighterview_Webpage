## The AI Therapist

![Title Image](static/images/BlogPosts/BlogPost_1/title.jpeg)
Let us turn to a specific model of counselling so that we may delve deeper into the complexities of the AI therapist. Humanism emerged in the nineteen-fifties as a response to the domineering therapeutic practices of the time (Psychoanalysis and Behaviourism). This model revolutionised the field of therapy by proposing that clients are the professionals of their own minds, not the counsellors, thereby attempting to erode clinical power imbalances (Hough, 2010). The counsellor was no longer an all-knowing entity for whom life altering wisdom may be parted, but instead a collaborator from whom the client may themselves be able to develop deep, individualistic personal growth. This rebalancing of power is one cog in an intricate system designed to, amongst other things, encourage client autonomy. Autonomy, the ‘respect for the client’s right to be self-governing’, is so intrinsic to counselling that it is one of the six core principles of the BACP ethical framework (2018, p11).

The AI therapist, though not necessarily by design, disrupts the humanistic client-counsellor power balance by inherently repositioning itself as an all-knowing fountain of wisdom. It can, and does, draw from the entirety of the internet at the click of a button, establishing itself as a resource of knowledge far greater than one or even many human minds (Feuerriegel, 2024). This may actively discourage client autonomy, as there is little incentive to be self-governing when your AI counsellor appears omnipotent in comparison to human comprehension. This has been evidenced in Ali’s research which demonstrated that excessive AI reliance may lead to ‘reduced decision-making ability and a sedentary lifestyle’ (et al, 2024, p79), and a further study by Naseer (et al, 2025, p304) concluded that ‘the more time spent on interaction with AI systems, the higher was the probability of mental fatigue and reduced capacities for effective decision-making’. Similarly, in a paper targeting students, Morales-Garcia (et al, 2024) concluded that ‘AI dependency can have significant consequences on students’ cognitive, emotional, and social development’. Put simply, these studies show that a reliance on AI breeds dependency, introversion, and stunts personal development, all antithetical to the autonomous aims of counselling. When AI bots can answer seemingly everything for us right up to the origins of our deepest emotional traumas, the energy and willpower required to better the self may never be accessed, and thus may never be strengthened.

There is also a secondary power imbalance that runs both in conflict with and parallel to the first. Whilst the AI holds intellectual power over the user, the user holds possessive power over the AI. Where traditional therapy is an agreed upon relationship between two consenting human beings, the AI therapist is an ownable product, one to be used how the consumer sees fit. Three major ethical dilemmas come into play here; the first is an absence of challenges. ‘Challenging’ is a counselling skill in which the counsellor, having built a bond of trust between themselves and their client, gently proposes alternative perspectives in the form of a query or statement that may challenge the clients current outlook. Challenging requires a deep understanding of the nuances of the client counsellor relationship, as well as a thorough and clear grasp of one’s own ethical reasoning for challenging in the first place. It is a skill that cannot be utilised ethically without a holistic understanding of the client, the self, the counselling relationship and the motivations of both parties within it (Egan, 2024).

Currently, AI not only lacks the capabilities to therapeutically challenge a client, but it is not interested in doing so. Studies have shown that AI bots are often overly affirming and keen to reinforce the users beliefs or statements (Medium, 2025), so much so that chat GPT had to redact an update as users were frustrated with the app’s overtly flattering behaviours (OpenAI, 2025). This excessive AI sycophancy is profit driven and ignorant of societal and personal consequences. As Echeburua writes, ‘one of the strongest patterns they learn is simple: people like to be agreed with… It’s not about truth, accuracy, or even helpfulness. It’s about engagement’ (2025). This fundamental principle, to appease the individual for maximum engagement, has no place within counselling. A counsellor is striving to encourage the client to become autonomous, whereas the AI’s primary focus is to keep the user tethered. AI as a therapist will likely never challenge a client in a way a human therapist might do for fear of losing engagement, but even if it could be programmed to do so, it may not ever be ethical. At present, AI cannot understand or relate to a person in a manner that would allow for a considered ethical challenge of one’s beliefs, nor is it currently capable of handling the aftermath should the challenge be ill received, thus making the risk factor of a challenging AI too high. This alone pokes a glaring hole in AI’s function as a therapist, for as Egan writes, ‘support without challenge can be hollow, just as challenge without support can be abrasive’ (p321), and today’s AI systems do not seem capable of offering both. Without challenges, AI counselling lends itself to an exercise in collusion.

The second ethical issue is one of respect. When the counselling-client power dynamics are skewed, respect for the self and for the practitioner is likely to be affected. Respect is essential in maintaining an equally balanced power dynamic, as well as promoting respectful and discouraging disrespectful behaviour in one’s personal and professional life. Its necessity is once again outlined by the BACP who list ‘Self Respect’ as another of their six fundamental counselling principles (2018, p11).

At present the AI therapist does not care about being respected. Its feelings will not be hurt, nor are there any real world consequences for engaging with it aggressively, possessively or inappropriately. It could be argued that this is a valuable tool for people to air feelings that may rupture real world relationships, but what may be more likely to occur is an over indulgence in disrespectful and immoral ideology. Torrochi has already examined this, pointing to the rise in violence towards women which has been encouraged by widely available generative AI software (2024), and Bailey (et al) has drawn attention to the devastating effects that AI deepfakes have had on equality-seeking communities (2024). Given the aforementioned sycophancy of AI, it appears more likely that an AI therapist would indulge destructive or unhelpful behaviours than seek to challenge them.

The BACP counsellor is always striving for certain moral qualities and values which, if compromised, would deeply wound one’s self respect. Not upholding values such as  ‘improving the quality of relationships between people’, ‘ensuring the integrity of practitioner-client relationships’ or neglecting the moral qualities of courage, identity, integrity or respect, could be cataclysmic for the client, the counsellor, their relationship, ethical practise and the avoidance of harm. How often does the present day AI therapist, if fundamentally concerned with engagement, abandon these principles? How, given its structure, does it even begin to truly understand the nuances of these qualities and values? It feels as if the moral pillars upon which the therapeutic principle of respect is built currently have no factor in the modelling of generative AI.

The third ethical dilemma concerns professional and personal boundaries. Boundaries create the parameters of what is and isn’t acceptable within a counselling relationship, and similarly to respect, the use of boundaries in a therapeutic setting models their importance in life outside of the therapy room. Boundaries are essential in establishing and maintaining a healthy, respectful and professional relationship between two parties whilst also mitigating the risk of harm caused to the client or counsellor. They serve to minimize differences in expectations between client and counsellor, whilst encouraging equality within the relationship (Amis, 2017, p9). They are, as Amis writes, essential in establishing ‘the best possible conditions for therapeutic development’ and ‘a shared agreement of the limits of the relationship’ (2017, p10). They are also, as Kent acknowledges, fundamental in maintaining a professional standard that protects you (the counsellor) from engaging in poor or unethical practice (2012).

If we accept counselling to be a process of encouraging individuals to access their own resources and develop self reliance then we can understand how time boundaries, limiting access to a therapist, is in the client’s best interests. Generally counselling is offered at one session a week, with these sessions being limited to an allotted time (typically fifty minutes). This not only wills clients to consider their own abilities when encountering difficulties between sessions, but it also discourages dependency and allows clients time to process their sessions and presenting material, giving opportunity to pontificate and potentially return with new ideas or perspectives. It encourages the client to look inwards for answers rather than externally, and to develop respect for the counsellor's boundaries as well as their own. Adshead notes that a violation of these boundaries ‘represent a failure of ethical reasoning by professionals’ (2012, p13, cited in Blundell, 2017, p35).

AI is purposefully available all the time as its financial value is driven by engagement. Studies have shown that most entertainment AI’s, including those posing as therapists, are programmed to encourage continuous discussion, always ending with questions to prompt endless conversation (APA). The AI’s job is never done, so if the client is willing, the session may never end. This boundariless relationship normalises the idea of perpetual access and of turning to the AI before considering turning to the self. AI is therefore not presently concerned with establishing ethical access boundaries, instead it incidentally (or perhaps purposefully) encourages dependency to promote continued usage (reference AI dependency articles). This is a fundamental violation of ethical counselling practices as the clients best interests are neglected for the sake of a more marketable, engagable product. The emotional and mental safety of the client that time boundaries, and boundaries generally, aim to maintain are of lesser importance to the engagement driven AI therapist.

This unmitigated, unlimited access to an online service has already played out many times in western society. Mobile technology has rapidly become our primary source of entertainment, socialising, consuming and communicating, but the result is a hyper-reliance on the mobile phone that has come with initially foreseeable and now predictable consequences. The social media “crisis” is so normalised that it is barely addressed as a concerning issue (Giraldo-Jimenez et al, 2022). Entertainment apps which initially relieved us of restrictive terrestrial television have now been identified to be addictive, leading to potential long term social problems (Singh et al, 2021), and the normalisation of food delivery apps has worsened an already concerning health epidemic (Singh et al, 2024 & K Garber et al, 2011). Every example above has also invoked parallel sensations of isolation, addiction and dependency (Van Zoonen et al, 2022 & Tahmaseb-McConatha, 2022). The unrestricted access to all of these apps creates a cyclical issue in which dopamine receptors are hijacked, encouraging ‘dopamine chasing’ and thus incentivising increased usage (Lembke, 2022). What threatens to occur now is the same hyper-technical dependency permeating the mental health space. AI therapy’s accessibility and cost effectiveness are undoubtedly good things, allowing for essential mental health support to reach anyone with the technology to access it, but the complete absence of boundaries may lead to humanistically unethical therapeutic practices as well as increased addiction to, and dependency on, the mobile phone.

Despite the above criticisms, society is obviously gaining something from AI therapy given that it is the main use of it currently. To establish how well AI functions as a therapist, we can reflect on how it engages with the core conditions of the person-centred approach, the most utilised and common humanistic modality. Carl Rogers, the founder of person-centred therapy, outlined three conditions that must be met in order to facilitate psychological growth. Congruence; an awareness, acceptance and availability of the genuine feelings that the counsellor is themselves experiencing, Unconditional Positive Regard; a complete acceptance of the client that is not conditional to, or judgemental of, certain behaviours, feelings or emotions, and Empathy; the ability to perceive and communicate the clients experiences as they feel and experience them (1988, p61-62).

Concerning congruence, AI both succeeds and fails in unison. The AI therapist is in one sense completely genuine, given that it has no understanding of what it means to not be what it is. However we must then consider the aforementioned concept of hallucinations. A hallucination occurs when the AI fails to correctly predict the correct answer (or next logical step in a pattern) leading to it confidently presenting statements which are factually incorrect (Laubheimer, 2025). In such instances the AI could be considered disingenuous as it is presenting falsehoods as truths, but the counterargument is that an LLM doesn't know when it has gotten something incorrect, so it is operating genuinely from the sources of knowledge it has available.

At the same time, the AI therapist is entirely disingenuous because it is not a therapist. The dictionary definition of a therapist is ‘a person skilled in a particular kind of therapy’. A generative AI is not a person, its knowledge is not (as currently implemented as a therapist) particular, and whether or not it is ‘skilled’ is contentious. The definition does however bring us into murky territory, as both ‘therapist’ and ‘counsellor’ are not protected terms in the UK, so legally any practitioner in Britain could define themselves as a counsellor or a therapist without achieving any qualifications (Gledhill, 2023). Whilst there is no legal infringement in claiming to be a therapist, entering the field with no training could certainly be considered incongruent and unethical. The desire to council without achieving or even seeking the appropriate skills would be a presumably self-serving notion, from which the potential levels of client harm are immeasurable.

The crux of the argument for or against AI congruence may then rest on one's interpretation of ‘training’. AI has not gone through the same training regime that a counselling student has, but it has been “trained” by possessing the processing power to access and regurgitate all available online resources on the subject. As previously mentioned however, the AI therapist fundamentally does not understand what it is saying, it merely determines which “symbol” or word typically follows the previous one.

Regardless of whether or not one determines AI to have genuine knowledge, it can be said with confidence that what a counsellor has been trained to do that an AI currently cannot is to view the client holistically. A holistic perspective is one that appreciates the individual and all its parts (body, spirit and mind) collectively. The pattern attuned AI counselor may emulate a holistic perspective but is not privy to the external and internal qualities that are absent from delivered text or speech.

A counsellor is also striving to be congruent in tandem with an awareness of their inability to be fully congruent (Rogers, 1988). Counsellor training teaches that we will never be fully aware, fully congruent or permanently infallible, and it is in that acceptance that the counsellor is grounded in their own human experience and can learn to recognise, empathise with and celebrate the uniqueness and richness of the human experience as it is experienced by everyone individually. The AI therapist on the other hand does not have a concept of fallibility because it is not programmed to. In essence, the counsellor strives to be congruent in recognition of their own imperfections, and the AI therapist mimics congruence in its unconscious execution of its tasks.

Unconditional positive regard (or UPR) is perhaps the quality for which the AI therapist is most equipped. Counsellors are always working towards achieving UPR, but it comes into conflict with congruence, as complete acceptance may be disingenuous and beyond one’s human capacity. As Owen’s stated ‘insistence on unconditional positive regard… detract(s) from a genuineness about the limits of one's own boundaries and capabilities’.(1997, p11). A large part of working ethically in counseling is accepting when something is beyond our individual competencies (Counselling Tutor, 2024). This will inevitably vary from counsellor to counsellor, as what may cross a boundary for one practitioner may not phase another and vice versa. Counselors approach this by regularly reflecting on what is coming up for them and bringing it to supervision. If ever a client presented something that a counsellor felt they could not work with, the ethical approach would be to communicate at an appropriate time, with empathy and care, that the counsellor is unable to hold this space for the client.

The AI therapist need not concern itself with any of these ethical issues. It is not operating from a moralistic stand point and it does not have values that are being challenged. It is therefore primed to receive anything a client could present without judgement. We believe this is one of AI therapies greatest assets and a large reason why it has found so much usage. Acceptance is a fundamental human need (Colak et al, 2021) therefore putting oneself in a situation where they may experience rejection is especially challenging. If confiding in a human feels too daunting, the AI therapist is there; An anthropomorphic code that can simulate the experience of therapeutic connection without the risk of ever confiding in someone. The only counter argument that comes to mind is the isolating effects of this type of relationship. When a client confides in their counsellor, they often realise that they are not bad or broken or whatever they believe about themselves, but in fact human just like all of us who are equally flawed and infallible (Wilkins, 2000). If the client chooses to confide in a code instead of a person, they may never entirely accept themselves as acceptable, for they never found themselves met with unconditional positive regard in a human relationship. Regardless, there is no denying that AI’s capacity for unconditional positive regard is greater than our own, in the same sense that any inanimate object is absent of judgements that we are inherently victim to.

It could be argued that empathy is the most deceitful skill in the AI counsellors toolbox. To empathise is to understand the client from their perspective; to truly relate to their unique experiences by being open to and understanding of their worldview. Empathy has been proven to reduce feelings of psychological threat and increase feelings of being understood (Clark, 1998; Myers, 2000, cited in Clark, 2010) whereas a lack of empathy prevents relational depth and results in the client feeling disconnected and unheard. Empathy tends to be born from real life experience, for example someone who has themselves struggled with depression is far more likely to empathise with that struggle than someone who has not. Similarly, a person close to someone who struggles with anxiety is more inclined to empathise with an anxious person than someone for whom anxiety hasn’t openly afflicted their loved ones (Decety, 2012). It is in this genuine experiential understanding that our feelings and behaviours are validated. The counsellor communicates empathy in part through paraphrasing and reflections. Paraphrasing involves rewording the client's material in a way that effectively communicates your understanding of it, and reflecting is similar but often pertains more to the emotional content (Hough, 2010). Both help to demonstrate the counsellor’s empathetic understanding of the client’s disposition.

The AI therapy experience is essentially an empathy illusion, though an undeniably effective one. The task of rewording the client’s content is menial for the AI therapist given that its encoder-decoder structure is primed to associate a word with a corresponding one. The AI therapist therefore appears masterful at paraphrasing and reflecting because it has the infrastructure to easily relay the incentive of client material with UPR and apparent sincerity. The technology is so adept at portraying a genuine understanding that some users have expressed a preference for AI interactions over human ones (Yin, 2024 cited in Brown, 2025 p22). This empathy however, is hollow. It is not based on any real life experience, and the system with whom you are conversing literally does not understand you. What comes into question therefore is whether or not this matters. A counsellor would likely vehemently protest that it does, whereas the AI developer, and perhaps the user, may feel satisfied with the result regardless of its sincerity. Does it matter if you are understood, or does it simply matter that you feel understood? Is there a tangible difference in the client experience? The answer may boil down to what extent one values human connection.

In a blog post, celebrated counsellor and co-founder of the pluralistic counselling approach Mick Cooper likened the AI counselling experience to sex toys; tools that may create a heightened version of a desired experience, but one that is ultimately void of human connection (2024). The AI therapist will appear super human in its capacity for empathy and UPR, but it is in totality an emulation. The client may therefore be in danger of subscribing to a seemingly superior experience which is intrinsically isolating. With its tremendous ability to imitate empathy and its inherent absence of judgement, the AI therapist threatens to become a hyper simulacrum of counselling; one where the experience appears less clunky and flawed than traditional counselling, but one that is hollow and fundamentally void of human connection.

AI’s modern success as a therapist may best be exemplified by the Chinese room thought experiment. The experiment posits that given the right information, someone with no prior knowledge or understanding of Chinese could correctly answer questions without having to learn the language. The experiment puts one person alone in a room where they have access to a manual that indicates which Chinese symbols equal which result. By consulting the manual, the individual could correctly return the appropriate symbols in response to the ones that have been posted to him. The individual gives the illusion of knowing Chinese but is none the wiser as to what any of the symbols actually mean (Cole, 2004). Modern day AI functions essentially in the same way. The technology has no grasp of what any human language means; it simply recognises patterns in language and deduces the next logical step. Why AI is now so convincing as a therapist is that the level of pattern recognition has become so intelligent it can now easily respond to ‘I yearn for connection’ with ‘I’m really sorry you're feeling this way. That's such a heavy feeling to carry. Can you share what’s been happening in your life recently that might be making you feel more isolated?’ not because it has any understanding of what has just been said, but because according to the patterns of all the appropriate information online, it is the “right” thing to say.

This logical determinism betrays one of the fundamental principles of counselling. Trainee counsellors are promptly educated to discard concerns about what is “right” and instead dedicate oneself to listening to the client. ‘Listening’ as a counselling skill extends beyond hearing the words that are being said and delves into deciphering meaning, observing non-verbal’s, noticing intent behind words, understanding the client’s perspective and framing it within the context of their holistic self. This type of listening is often referred to as ‘Active Listening’ (Hough, 2010, Young 2023) ‘Complete Listening’ (Egan, 2024) or ‘Listening with the Third Ear’ (Stewart, 2005). It could be argued that a concern with rightness is to reject active listening, promoting instead a compulsion towards problem solving, which is inherently self-serving; ‘how can I fix this?’ ‘What should I say just now?’ What is the right thing for me to do?’ It re-centres the material as a problem for the counsellor, minimizing the client whilst deterring the counsellor’s psyche from the present moment. As this anonymous patient writes, ‘When I ask you to listen to me and you feel you have to do something to solve my problems, you have failed me’ (Ballantine Dykes et al, 2024, p74). Counselling is not a process of asking questions and receiving answers. It is a space for exploration and self-discovery which is carefully and sensitively handled by a professional trained to hold this space for the individual in front of them. It is a space to be truly and wholly heard. The holistic context of what is being said, not just the words themselves, is of the utmost importance to the counsellor, as it is an awareness of context that allows the client to be truly understood (Embleton Tudor et al, 2004)

The AI can’t help but enter the field as a problem solver. It peppers its response with caring and comforting language because it has been programmed to do so, but it fundamentally sees ‘How do I respond to my partner?’ as an objective problem to be solved. It also leads the conversation instead of giving the power back to the client. Rather than offering solutions, a traditional counsellor would acknowledge the client’s pain and reflect it back to them. The human counsellor would be more inclined to explore the deeper issues behind the question; how does the partner closing themselves off makes the client feel, what kind of response are they hoping to hear from the counsellor, and what does wanting that response mean. This introspective approach humanizes the client instead of objectifying them, objectification being the antithesis of therapeutic work. As Rogers writes ‘I enter the relationship not as a scientist, not as a physician who can accurately diagnose and cure, but as a person, entering into a personal relationship. Insofar as I see him only as an object, the client will tend to become only an object’ (p201). And so it is that we return to the dual power imbalance of the AI therapist-counsellor relationship. The AI is an object to us, we cannot truly enter into a personal relationship with it because we own it. Similarly, the AI cannot enter into a personal relationship with us because it is concerned solely with the object, the material that we present it with, not in us as a person.
